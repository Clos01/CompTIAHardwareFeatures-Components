    Section 4: Notes

  • CPU (Central Processing Unit): is the "brain" of the computer, managing tasks and instructions. It tells other components what to do and when to do it.
    • processes multiple tasks at once, making it like a super-fast calculator.
  • CPU Sockets: Sockets are flat with rows of holes or pins where the CPU connects to the motherboard.
    • PGA (Pin Grid Array): The processor has pins that fit into holes in the socket.
    • LGA (Land Grid Array): The socket has contacts that connect to the processor's flat contacts.
    • More complex locking mechanisms are used.
    • BGA (Ball Grid Array): Uses tiny soldered balls as contacts. Generally, the processor is permanently soldered onto the motherboard and is not replaceable.
  • RAM (Random Access Memory):
    • RAM stores currently used data and instructions for the CPU, speeding up access to frequently used data.
    • Types of RAM include DDR2, DDR3, and DDR4.
    • DIMM (Dual Inline Memory Module): Used in desktops.
    • SODIMM (Small Outline DIMM): A smaller form of RAM used in laptops.
  • Cache Memory: Cache is a small, fast memory between the CPU and RAM.
    • Stores frequently accessed data, prefetching it for the CPU to improve performance.
    • Cache memory cannot be removed or upgraded like RAM.

    Section 5: Expansion slots and disk controllers

    Section 5 : Recall

    • Expansion Slots
    • PCI (Peripheral Component Interconnect)
    • AGP (Accelerated Graphics Port)
    • PCIe (PCI Express)

    Relevant Questions

    • What are expansion slots on a motherboard, and what are they used for?
    • What is PCI, and what are its characteristics?
    • What are AGP expansion slots, and what are they used for?
    • What makes PCIe slots the most common expansion slot in modern motherboards?

    Section 5: Notes

  • Expansion Slots: Small plastic slots on the motherboard that allow users to install various devices, such as video, network, sound, and disk interface cards.
    • These slots expand the capabilities of a computer by adding new hardware components.
  • PCI (Peripheral Component Interconnect): 32-bit slots, typically around 3 inches long.
    • Operate at 33 MHz or 66 MHz, with data rates up to 133 Mbps.
    • Handle both 3.3V and 5V adapters.
  • AGP (Accelerated Graphics Port):
    • Designed for video cards, providing a direct connection between video circuitry and the computer’s memory.
    • AGP slots are slightly shorter than PCI slots and are typically brown.
  • PCIe (PCI Express): The most common and fastest expansion slot in modern motherboards.
    • Supports speeds ranging from x1 to x32 lanes.
    • PCIe 4.0 x16 slots can reach speeds up to 31.5 Gbps, making them ideal for high-performance applications, such as gaming and advanced video editing

    Section 6: Disk Controllers

    Section 6 : Recall

    • Disk Controllers
    • IDE (Integrated Drive Electronics or PATA)
    • AGP (Accelerated Graphics Port)
    • SATA (Serial ATA)

    Relevant Questions

    • What is the primary function of disk controllers in a computer?
    • What is the difference between IDE (PATA) and SATA hard drive connectors?

    Section 6: Notes

  • Disk Controllers: Manage the connection between the computer's motherboard and the hard drive.
    • Allow data to be stored and retrieved from the hard drive.
  • IDE (PATA) vs. SATA:
    • IDE (PATA): Older standard known as Parallel ATA, uses large ribbon-like cables.
    • SATA: A newer, faster standard known as Serial ATA, which uses smaller and more efficient cables, making the connection faster and more reliable.

    • Section 7: BIOS and POST

    Section 7 : Recall

    • Firmware
    • BIOS (Basic Input/Output System)
    • POST (Power-On Self Test)
    • ROM (Read-Only Memory)

    Relevant Questions

    • What is BIOS, and what is its role in a computer?
    • Where does the BIOS come from?
    • Is POST a chip?
    • What is firmware, and how does it differ from software?

    Section 7: Notes

  • Firmware: Firmware is software embedded in hardware, often in a ROM chip, and it functions without additional instructions from the operating system.
  • BIOS (Basic Input/Output System):
    • A type of firmware built into the motherboard, responsible for starting the system and running initial checks (POST)
      • Tests system components such as CPU, memory, and storage and loads the operating system.
    • SATA: A newer, faster standard known as Serial ATA, which uses smaller and more efficient cables, making the connection faster and more reliable.
  • POST (Power-On Self Test):
    • Not a chip, but part of the BIOS firmware.
      • Runs checks when the computer boots up to ensure that all hardware components are functioning properly.
  • BIOS Chip:
    • The BIOS firmware is stored on a chip on the motherboard, provided by manufacturers like AMI, Phoenix, or Award.
  • Flashing the BIOS:
    • When hardware is upgraded but not recognized by the BIOS, it might require a BIOS update through a process called "flashing the BIOS."

    Section 7: Notes

  • CMOS: Stores important system settings (date, time, hard drive configuration, CPU settings) on the motherboard
    • It requires constant power, which is provided by the CMOS battery, to retain settings when the computer is off.
  • CMOS vs. BIOS:
    • A type of firmware built into the motherboard, responsible for starting the system and running initial checks (POST)
  • Back-Panel Connectors:
    • Ports located at the back of the computer for peripherals such as power, video, audio, and networking devices.
  • Front-Panel Connectors:
    • Ports on the front of the computer for convenient access to USB devices, audio jacks, and more.
  • Common front-panel connectors include:
    • Power Button: Turns the computer on and off.
    • Power Button: Turns the computer on and off.
    • Reset Button: Restarts the computer without turning off the power.
    • Drive Activity Lights: Show when the hard drive is active.
    • Audio Ports: Connect headphones and microphones.
    • USB Ports: For external devices like flash drives.

    Section 9: Processors

    Section 9 : Recall

    • Silicon Chip
    • Transistor
    • PGA (Pin Grid Array)
    • LGA (Land Grid Array)
    • Heat Sink

    Relevant Questions

    • What are processors made of, and who are the major manufacturers?
    • What is the difference between PGA and LGA sockets?
    • Why is heat management important for processors, and what systems are used?

    Section 7: Notes

  • Processor (CPU): Made of silicon chips containing millions of transistors.
    • Major manufacturers include Intel and AMD.
  • PGA vs. LGA Sockets:
    • PGA (Pin Grid Array): Pins are located on the processor and fit into holes on the motherboard
  • LGA (Land Grid Array)
    • Pins are located on the motherboard, making the processor more robust and reducing the chance of damage to the processor.
  • Heat Management:
    • Processors generate heat and need cooling systems, such as heat sinks and fans, to prevent overheating.
    • A heat sink consists of metal fins that draw heat away from the processor.
    • A fan blows air over the heat sink to cool it down.
